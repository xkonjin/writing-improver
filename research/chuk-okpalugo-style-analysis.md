## The Core Technique: Diagnosis, Not Description

Chuk doesn't write explainers. He writes **strategic diagnoses**. Every news item gets framed through competitive dynamics and strategic implications. He replaces "here's what happened" with "here's what this actually means."

| Boring Explainer                              | Chuk's Version                                                                                      |
| --------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| "Bridge launched stablecoin issuance APIs"    | "Bridge's launch was drowned in the sea of announcements but is worth double-clicking on"           |
| "Stablecoins could help Western Union"        | "Stablecoins won't save Western Union's past, but they can fund its future"                         |
| "Banks should be concerned about stablecoins" | "Banks are not being disintermediated by interest rates. They are being outcompeted on usefulness." |
| "There are many new stablecoins launching"    | "How many stablecoins are too many?"                                                                |

## Hook Formulas

1. **Narrative moment** — "For a brief moment on July 22nd, it seemed like Western Union might be back..."
2. **Provocative question** — "How many stablecoins are too many?"
3. **Contrarian signal** — "X is NOT about Y, it's about Z"
4. **Buried-lede recovery** — "This was drowned in the sea of announcements but is worth double-clicking on"
5. **Bold thesis upfront** — "Stablecoin issuance is becoming commoditized infrastructure. It is no longer a high-friction product."

## Specific Techniques

### A. The "Double-Click"

Starts with a news event, then signals the surface read is wrong: "worth double-clicking on." Creates a knowledge gap.

### B. Thesis-First Structure

States thesis boldly in first paragraph, then builds the case. Opposite of explainer structure (which buries the conclusion).

### C. Contrarian Framing

Almost every piece challenges consensus:

- "Stablecoin interest is NOT the biggest threat facing bank deposits" — the threat is utility
- "This is not about a new feature. It is about lowering the barrier to entry."

### D. Numbers as Punctuation

Drops in "$200B+" or "400,000 physical agent locations" as single proof points, not data walls.

### E. Familiar-to-Unfamiliar Bridging

"This mirrors how a dollar on CashApp isn't directly fungible with one on Venmo" — anchors new concepts in everyday experience.

### F. Frameworks Over Features

Instead of listing features, he categorizes: "SWIFT in messaging, Visa in vetting." One framework replaces paragraphs.

### G. Blunt Conclusions

No "time will tell." He stakes a position:

- "Banks are being outcompeted on usefulness."
- "Deposit erosion follows functionality, not fear."

## Tone

- **Confident and declarative** — no hedging with "perhaps" or "it remains to be seen"
- **Strategic, not technical** — explains what it means for the market, not how the tech works
- **Conversational but not slangy** — "double-click on," "I'm here for it"
- **Constructive contrarian** — challenges consensus but always offers the better framing

### Newsletter — Strategic Implications

"Meta exploring stablecoin payouts on Instagram would be a very practical move. Unlike the abandoned Libra project, this approach focuses on a clear, high-impact use case: paying creators globally."

Then the numbers: "Instagram supports ~20 countries with a $25 minimum; TikTok has a $10 threshold but in fewer regions. These limits exclude micro-earners. Stablecoins make even $5 bonuses viable."

Then the escalation: "It's also a strategic beachhead. If successful, the same rails could support merchant payments, P2P transfers, and marketplace escrow across Meta's platforms."

### American Banker — The Contrarian Argument

"The real competitive threat to U.S. banks is not stablecoin yield itself, but the steady rise of wallet apps and fintech platforms."

Then: "Yield can attract deposits, but utility determines where deposits ultimately stay."

Historical pattern: Banks have lost primacy "in remittances. Then in peer-to-peer payments. Then in retail trading and investing."

Close: "Banks are not being disintermediated by interest rates. They are being outcompeted on usefulness."

## The Key Lesson for Jin's Content

Chuk's content has STAKES. Companies will die, others will dominate, the map is being redrawn. He writes about who's winning, who's losing, and what moves are being made RIGHT NOW.

An explainer says: "Layer 3 is banking rails — you need bank accounts in every jurisdiction."
Chuk would say: "The list of banks willing to touch stablecoin flows is shorter than anyone admits. The companies that locked in those relationships 2 years ago now have pricing power over everyone trying to enter."

The difference: one is information. The other is intelligence.
